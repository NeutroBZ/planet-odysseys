<p style="text-align: center;">Taquile Island is popular for its textile creativity and clothing. One of the most fascinating <b>Taquile island facts </b>is that in the year 2005, Taquile's workmanship was considered crucial to such an extent that <a href="https://ich.unesco.org/en/RL/taquile-and-its-textile-art-00166">UNESCO</a> regarded it as a metaphysical Cultural Heritage of Humanity.</p><p style="text-align: center;">Here on this island females weave and monitor the sheep that provide the yarn. Men here produce the island’s sewn caps. Alejandro – one of the 7 men on the island distinguished as a Master of Textiles. Alongside the leader of the island Juan Quispe Huatta.</p><p><img class="aligncenter wp-image-8071 size-full" src="/images/blog/taquile-and-its-textile-art-where-manliness-is-based-on-knitting/Taquile.png" alt="Taquile Island" width="800" height="482" /></p><h4 style="text-align: center;"><b>History of the Textile Art: </b></h4><p style="text-align: center;">The tradition of textile art is around 500 years old. The ancient civilizations of the Pukara, Inca, and Colla peoples ground its roots. Even on this small Peruvian island, the men have attracted their partners by knitting elaborate Andean hats.</p><p style="text-align: center;">The 67-year-old Alejandro Flores Huatta understood how to knit the iconic chullo (which refers to a tall, floppy Andean hat) when he was a child. He learned this art from his grandfather and elder brother. They used to teach knitting by using the thorns of a cactus as a needle.</p><p style="text-align: center;">Another interesting <b>Taquile island fact </b>is that here the men learn knitting by glancing and observing their elders or other experienced people.</p><p><img class="aligncenter wp-image-8072 size-full" src="/images/blog/taquile-and-its-textile-art-where-manliness-is-based-on-knitting/Taquile.jpeg" alt="Taquile Island" width="800" height="533" /></p><h4 style="text-align: center;"><b>Island’s Men's Creativity, Dreams, and Aspirations: </b></h4><p style="text-align: center;">You may be unaware of the <b>Taquile island fact </b>that here men's ability or worth is assessed not on how well they can hunt, or catch fish but rather it is being estimated on their proficiency to knit.  The males of Taquile Island learn knitting from the very tender age of five or six. One male individual passes on this skill to another. Also, the first chullo a boy knits must be white later on he can dye threads when he is able to knit a neat and tight hat.</p><p style="text-align: center;">The Chullos or tall Andean hats are culturally crucial, playing a major part in the island's social structure. These Chullos take a month to be completed as it has intricate patterns and certain iconography that indicates seasonal, agricultural, and familial totems.</p><p style="text-align: center;"> It is an opportunity for men to showcase their inventiveness while also illustrating their dreams, marital status, and aspirations – a few men use it to express their moods. Females select their male partners based on the male's mastery in knitting a chullo with wire-thin needles.
